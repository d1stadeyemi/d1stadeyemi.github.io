Gut Microbiota Secondary Metabolites: Key Roles in GI Tract Cancers and Infectious Diseases

Muhammad Ajagbe et al.

This review examines how the gut microbiota produces secondary metabolites — including short-chain fatty acids, indoles, and bile acids — that modulate immune responses and epigenetic mechanisms. In gastrointestinal cancers (colorectal, liver, gastric), these metabolites can drive tumorigenesis through inflammation, DNA damage, and immune evasion, while also holding therapeutic promise by enhancing chemotherapy and immunotherapy responses. The review further explores their role in infectious disease resilience and calls for deeper research into microbiome-based therapeutic interventions.

Metagenomic Profiling of Antimicrobial Resistance in Siwa Oasis Therapeutic Hot Springs

Muhammad Ajagbe, Ali H. A. Elbehery, Shimaa F. Ahmed, Amged Ouf, Basma M. T. Abdoullateef, Rehab Abdallah, and Rania Siam

Using high-resolution NovaSeq-6000 shotgun sequencing, this study delivers a comprehensive metagenomic characterization of two historically therapeutic hot springs — Cleopatra and Fatnas — in Siwa Oasis, Egypt. From over 37 reconstructed metagenome-assembled genomes (MAGs), the analysis identified six antimicrobial resistance genes spanning aminoglycoside, beta-lactam, fluoroquinolone, and rifamycin resistance classes, alongside 11 virulence factors linked to adhesion, invasion, and immune modulation. Notably, a non-tuberculous Mycobacterium ahvazicum MAG harbored ∼50% of these resistance and virulence signatures — a clinically relevant finding in a human-frequented environment. The work demonstrates end-to-end bioinformatics capacity: from raw sequencing data through quality control, taxonomic profiling, MAG binning, phylogenomics, and resistance gene annotation at scale.
